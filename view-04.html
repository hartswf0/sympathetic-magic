Foundational Concepts: Frazer's Laws

James George Frazer's Golden Bough (1889) identified two foundational principles of sympathetic magic:

[similarity] ("like affects like"): The magician infers that by imitating an effect, they produce it. A figure resembling a person can affect that person through likeness.

[association] ("contact leaves trace"): Whatever touches an object transfers essence. "Once in contact, always in contact." Fingernail parings, hair, clothing—all carry the essence of their original possessor.

These are not primitive superstitions but persistent human cognitive patterns. Rozin et al. (2005) demonstrated that even Ivy League students refused to eat fudge shaped like dog feces or wear sweaters previously worn by AIDS patients—sympathetic magic persists in modernity.

The ethical stakes: if likeness carries power and contact leaves permanent traces, then every representation—every photograph, video, data profile, documentary—is an act of magical transfer with real consequences.

Photography & Capture: Sontag, Barthes, Violence

Theory

Susan Sontag: "To photograph people is to violate them." Every photograph is an act of possession. The camera is a weapon that reduces subjects to images. Repeated exposure to violent photographs creates moral numbing—we become immune to atrocity.

Roland Barthes: Photography splits into studium (conscious engagement) and punctum (that which pierces us). The punctum is often traumatic—the detail that wounds, the moment of death, the vulnerability exposed.

The ethical crisis: photographs circulate endlessly, divorced from context, stripped of consent.

The image then circulates, touching millions of viewers, each of whom carries a fragment of the subject's essence. The original subject can never fully reclaim their image.

Digital Identity & Contagion

Your digital identity is a shadow self—a voodoo doll made of data. Platforms collect fragments: browsing history, location data, social connections, biometric markers. These traces [persist] and [associate], creating a profile that haunts you across platforms.

[similarity] principle: Your digital avatar resembles you through consistent identifiers (usernames, profile pictures). Platforms use this likeness to predict your behavior, target you with content, shape your perception.

[association] principle: One platform's data "touches" another through APIs, data brokers, third-party integrations. Contact leaves permanent trace. Your Facebook profile bleeds into your LinkedIn profile bleeds into your credit profile.

USER DATA CONTAGION NETWORK: [Personal Info] ──→ [Platform A] ──→ [Data Broker] ──→ [Platform B] ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ [Digital Self] [Collection] [Aggregation] [Profile Prediction] ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ [Behavior] [Analysis] [Packaging] [Targeting] ↓ ↓ ↓ ↓ [Real World] ←──── [Sale] ←───── [Distribution] ←── [Use] Once collected, your data can never be fully erased. This is the permanent essence transfer.

Concrete Scenario:

  1. You disclose location data to Google Maps (contact)
  2. Google correlates this with search history, email, YouTube watch time (similarity)
  3. This profile is sold to advertisers, insurers, employers (association)
  4. You're denied credit or employment based on inferred attributes (effect)

Your digital traces have material consequences in the physical world. The voodoo doll made of your data can affect your actual body—where you can live, what you can buy, whom you can love.

Circulation & Poor Images (Steyerl)

Hito Steyerl argues that modern images are "copies in motion"—degraded, compressed, ripped, remixed, and re-posted across platforms. The poor image is not a failure; it's an index of circulation and resistance to commodity value.

Yet mimesis can also weaponize. Dominant cultures use mimicry to absorb and neutralize subaltern voices.

Documentary Ethics & The Right to Represent

ETHICAL DILEMMA: A filmmaker wants to tell the story of a marginalized community experiencing violence. Three paths:

PATH A: Informed Consent
  • Get written permission from all subjects
  • Share footage, discuss representation choices
  • Allow subjects to veto or request changes
  • Offer compensation for participation
  • Result: Slower, more collaborative, subjects have agency

Source: Documentary Ethics Guidelines (POV, ITVS)

PATH B: Participatory/Co-Created
  • Subjects help shape narrative
  • Shared decision-making throughout production
  • Subjects see rough cuts, provide feedback
  • Credits list subjects as creative contributors
  • Result: Deeper authenticity, shared authorship

Source: Fader, "What to Do About Documentary Distortion" (2024)

PATH C: Observational/Hidden
  • Filmmaker captures "reality" without interference
  • Justification: Truth requires authenticity, subjects might perform
  • Risk: Violates consent, exploits vulnerability, perpetuates harm
  • Outcome: Subjects see themselves represented without input, may feel betrayed

Source: Documentary Ethics Code, Jon Else on filmmaker responsibility

The Central Tension:

Power imbalance between filmmaker (who controls representation) and subjects (whose story is being told). Ethical practice requires acknowledging this imbalance and ceding some control.

Transparency About Construction:

  • Document all decisions: casting choices, editing decisions, what was left out
  • Show alternative cuts showing how editing shapes narrative
  • Include subject interviews discussing their representation
  • Archive: Interview transcripts, filming permits, editing logs—make these public

Operational Ethics (morphisms you can act on):

  • [contextualize]: Give subjects' full stories, not decontextualized clips
  • [consent-seek]: Obtain informed, ongoing consent
  • [trace]: Document provenance of footage, permissions granted
  • [repair]: Offer takedowns or reshoots if harm occurs
  • [pluralize]: Include dissenting voices, internal disagreements

Algorithms & Collective Memory

How Algorithms Shape What We Remember:

Recommendation algorithms (on YouTube, TikTok, Facebook, Instagram) don't just show you content—they fundamentally reshape collective memory by deciding which historical narratives reach which audiences.

Algorithm Logic (Simplified):

  1. User engagement as signal: Likes, shares, watch time are treated as indicators of content value
  2. Similarity matching: Users with similar engagement patterns see similar content (filter bubbles)
  3. Optimization for engagement: Algorithm maximizes for "meaningful social interactions"
  4. Result: Sensational, emotionally resonant content gets amplified; nuanced, contextual content gets buried
ALGORITHMIC MEMORY CURATION: [Historical Event] → [Multiple Narratives] ↓ ↓ [Archive A] [Archive B] [Archive C] [Archive D] ↓ ↓ [Upload to Platform] ← Algorithm filters ← [Engagement metrics] ↓ ↓ [High Engagement] ────────────────→ [AMPLIFIED] [Low Engagement] ────────────────→ [BURIED] ↓ ↓ [Viral Reach] [Obscurity] ↓ ↓ [Collective Memory] ←───────────── [What we "know"]

Case Study: Holocaust Memory on TikTok

Research by Makhortykh and others (2023-2024) shows:

  • Holocaust memorial organizations post educational content
  • Posts featuring survivor testimony get MORE engagement than posts about historical context or memorial sites
  • Algorithm amplifies the most engaging survivor content → collective memory becomes focused on individual testimony rather than institutional history
  • Problem: Survivors become "the story" rather than ONE part of larger narrative
  • TikTok's algorithm also tags Holocaust-related content alongside Holocaust denial and distortion (same algorithmic category) → these false narratives spread alongside legitimate education

The #WeRemember Campaign (2021):

  • Holocaust memorial organizations collaborated with TikTok at corporate level
  • Used matrix approach: multiple high-profile accounts shared synchronized commemorative messages
  • Corporate/algorithmic partnership allowed manipulation of algorithm to ensure Holocaust education content received massive reach
  • Positive outcome: Reached younger audiences who wouldn't encounter this content otherwise
  • Ethical question: Is corporate manipulation of algorithm justified if outcome is educational?

Effects of Algorithmic Curation on Collective Memory:

  1. Amplification: Certain narratives reach millions; others reach dozens
  2. Decontextualization: Images and quotes circulate without source information
  3. Homogenization: Different communities' historical narratives get flattened into algorithmic categories
  4. Temporal distortion: Past events circulate as if happening now (decontextualizing trauma, making it "viral")
  5. Power concentration: Platforms decide what counts as historical knowledge

Ethical Implications:

  • Algorithms are not neutral; they embody values and power relations
  • Collective memory is no longer just shaped by historians, archivists, and communities—it's shaped by engagement metrics
  • We need algorithmic transparency: Make recommendation logic visible, auditable, contestable
  • We need memory governance: Communities should have voice in how their histories are algorithmically curated
